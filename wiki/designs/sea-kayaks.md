# Sea kayaks

The general-purpose decked ocean/coastal kayak. A huge and varied category — "sea kayak" covers everything from 3.8 m play boats to 5.8 m expedition tanks.

This page is the **broad overview**. Specific subcategories get their own pages:
- [touring.md](touring.md) — long-distance expedition.
- [coaster-analysis.md](coaster-analysis.md) — a specific short-play example.
- [ursa-and-mariner.md](ursa-and-mariner.md) — a design family.

---

## Defining features

- Fully decked (closed top) with a cockpit.
- Typically two watertight compartments (fore and aft hatches with bulkheads).
- Deck lines on both decks.
- Skeg or rudder for directional control.
- Designed for open water: ocean, big lakes, exposed coast.
- Paddler sits in a molded seat with thigh braces for edging.
- Typically uses a spray skirt.
- LOA generally 3.8–5.8 m; beam 0.50–0.62 m; L/B 8–11.

---

## Subcategories

### Day boat / play boat
- 3.8–4.6 m, moderate beam (0.56–0.62).
- More rocker → easier turning.
- Smaller hatch volume.
- **Mariner Coaster (3.81 m) is a canonical example.** See [coaster-analysis.md](coaster-analysis.md).
- Other examples: Valley Avocet, Pintail, Nigel Dennis Romany Surf, P&H Delphin.

### British-style touring
- 4.9–5.4 m, narrow (0.53–0.57).
- Moderate-low rocker → good tracking.
- Historically fiberglass, often with Greenland influence.
- Emphasize "playability in rough water" — rolling, bracing, edge-turning.
- Examples: Nigel Dennis Explorer, Valley Nordkapp, P&H Cetus, Rockpool GT.

### North American / expedition
- 5.3–5.8 m, slightly wider (0.56–0.62).
- Larger hatch volume for multi-week trips.
- Often plastic (Necky, Current Designs) or composite (CD, Seaward).
- Examples: Current Designs Solstice, Necky Looksha, Seaward Tyee. See [touring.md](touring.md).

### Greenland-style
- Explicitly referencing traditional qajaq proportions.
- 5.0–5.3 m, very narrow (0.47–0.53), low sheer, low deck.
- Hard or soft chines; low afterdeck for laybacks.
- Examples: Valley Anas Acuta, Tahe Greenland, Sterling Reflection.

### Surfski-adjacent fast tourers
- 5.3–5.8 m, narrow (0.50–0.55).
- Low rocker, fine entry.
- Cross between a sea kayak and a surfski. Examples: Epic 18X, Stellar ST18, Kirton Inuk.

## What sea kayaks optimize for

Unlike K1s and surfskis, sea kayaks balance **multiple** priorities. A good sea kayak is not the fastest, most stable, or most maneuverable — it is the *best compromise* for its intended use.

### Common priorities
- **Tracking** — ability to hold a straight course without constant correction.
- **Seaworthiness** — behavior in chop, surf, wind.
- **Capacity** — enough volume to carry camping gear.
- **Maneuverability** — ability to turn quickly when needed (surf zones, rock gardens).
- **Rolling** — to recover from capsize.
- **Efficiency** — cruise drag at 2.0–2.5 m/s.
- **Stability** — enough to be relaxing, not so much that it costs everything else.

### The trade-off lattice

| Want more of... | Pay in... |
|---|---|
| Speed | Beam (narrower is faster → less stable), length (longer is faster → more WSA at low speed → harder to store/car-top), rocker (less is faster → less maneuverable) |
| Tracking | Rocker reduction → maneuverability loss |
| Turning | More rocker → tracking loss |
| Stability | Beam → drag |
| Capacity | Beam → drag (and width → performance) |
| Rolling | Low deck, low volume — conflicts with capacity |
| Surf play | Rocker, short LWL — conflicts with speed |

Every sea kayak is a specific point in this tradeoff space.

| Metric | Typical range |

| LOA | 4.5 – 5.8 m |
| LWL | 4.2 – 5.4 m |
| Beam | 0.50 – 0.62 m |
| BWL | 0.48 – 0.58 m |

| Draft | 0.08 – 0.14 m |
| Displacement (with paddler+gear) | 100 – 200 kg |
| GM_T | 0.05 – 0.20 m |
| AVS | 90° – 140° |
| Rocker (total, bow+stern) | 0.04 – 0.15 m |

---

## Drag signature

Paddlers cruise at 1.8 – 2.8 m/s (Fr 0.25–0.38). Most sea kayaks:
- Frictional drag dominates at cruise.
- Wave-making rises in the 0.35–0.40 Fr region.
- Above 3 m/s, drag climbs steeply for most hulls.

A longer, narrower sea kayak (Epic 18X, Stellar ST18) will have a flatter drag curve at cruise and accept more WSA to get it. A shorter, rockered play boat (Coaster, Romany Surf) will be higher-drag at cruise but more fun in surf and easier to turn.

---

## Stability signature

Sea kayaks are designed for *paddler-assisted* stability. The paddle provides lateral bracing; the boat doesn't need high initial GM_T. In fact too-high GM_T makes the boat "tippy-feeling" in waves because it forces the boat to follow wave slope exactly.

A typical sea-kayak GZ curve:
- GM_T moderate (0.08–0.15 m).
- Secondary stability peak at 25–40° heel, then declining.
- AVS 100–140° depending on deck volume.
- Cockpit flooding angle typically past 90° for closed-deck designs.

---

## British vs North American design schools

A caricature but useful:

- **British** school (Goodman, Dennis, Foster, Bjørn Thomasson): lower deck, narrower, Greenland-adjacent, emphasis on playability and on-water technique. More edging, less stability.
- **North American** school (Broze / Mariner, Moyer / PWS, early CD): often shorter, slightly beamier, focused on realistic-conditions seaworthiness for intermediate paddlers. Soft chines, moderate stability.

Both schools have produced iconic boats.

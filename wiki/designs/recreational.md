# Recreational kayaks

The entry-level category. Short, beamy, stable, cheap, forgiving. Designed for the beginner paddling on a lake on a sunny afternoon.

---

## Typical numbers

| Variable | Value |
|---|---|
| LOA | 3.0 – 4.0 m |
| Beam | 0.65 – 0.80 m |
| L/B | 4 – 5 |
| Depth | 0.30 – 0.40 m |
| Cockpit | Large, often open-keyhole |
| Material | Almost always rotomolded polyethylene |
| Mass | 18 – 30 kg |

---

## What they optimize

- **Initial stability.** Wide, flat-ish bottoms. Paddler can sit still without conscious balance.
- **Easy entry/exit.** Large cockpit.
- **Affordability.** Rotomolded plastic, minimal finishing.
- **Durability.** They'll get dragged on beaches.

Everything else — speed, tracking, maneuverability, rough-water handling — is sacrificed or limited.

---

## What they are not

- Not seaworthy. No bulkheads (often), high cockpit, low hatch volume, poor wave handling.
- Not fast. L/B of 4 means enormous wave-making drag at any reasonable speed.
- Not maneuverable relative to a whitewater kayak. Moderate rocker at best.
- Not efficient for distance.

Recreational kayaks are rentals, first-kayaks, and casual-paddle tools. That's fine — they're a huge slice of the market.

---

## Representative models

Many production kayak companies make these: Wilderness Systems Pungo/Aspire, Dagger Zydeco/Stratos, Old Town Loon, Perception Prodigy, Pelican (budget), Current Designs Kestrel, Eddyline Sky, etc.

---

## Why we support designing them anyway

Even a recreational kayak is a hull. The same ArcasBoat primitives — NURBS surface, hydrostatics, stability, drag — apply. A designer should be able to build a 3.5 m × 0.72 m "entry-level hull" and look at its numbers the same way they look at a K1's.

If ArcasBoat is successful for pro designers, it'll also be a useful educational tool for understanding *why* recreational kayaks have the hydrostatic signatures they do.

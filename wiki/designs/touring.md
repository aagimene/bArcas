# Touring / expedition kayaks

Subcategory of sea kayak optimized for **multi-day cargo capacity** and **tracking over long distances**. Not always clearly distinguished from general sea kayaks — the distinction is one of emphasis.

---

## What makes a touring kayak distinct

- **Larger cargo volume** — typically 150–250 L of hatch space, sometimes more.
- **Longer LWL** — 4.9–5.4 m typical.
- **Slightly wider beam** for capacity — 0.55–0.62 m, but narrow enough to remain efficient.
- **Moderate rocker biased toward tracking** — less rocker than a play boat, enough that the boat handles in waves.
- **Heavier displacement** — loaded with gear, the design condition is often 120–200 kg.
- **Skeg or rudder** — usually one or the other; helpful for crosswind handling when loaded.

---

## Representative designs

- **Valley Nordkapp** (Frank Goodman, 1975+) — the classic. 5.50 m, 0.54 m beam. British expedition reference. Skeg-equipped. Several revisions (Nordkapp LV, Nordkapp Forti).
- **Nigel Dennis Explorer** — 5.38 m, 0.55 m beam. Benchmark British expedition boat.
- **P&H Cetus** — modern British expedition kayak in several sizes (MV, LV, HV).
- **Current Designs Solstice / Titan** — North American expedition boats, plastic and composite.
- **Necky Looksha Sport / Looksha IV** — tough plastic expedition kayaks.
- **Seaward Tyee / Passat** — British Columbia expedition builders.
- **Tiderace Xcite / Xplore** — modern UK designs.
- **Epic 18X** — fast expedition-ish tourer, slightly Surfski-flavored.
- **Stellar ST19** — another fast-tourer.

## Design tradeoffs specific to expedition

### Load-sensitive hydrostatics
An empty expedition kayak has very different waterline than a fully loaded one. Designers often target the **loaded condition** as primary and accept some performance loss when empty. Good expedition designs are stable and trackable across a 40–50 kg load variation.

### Symmetric loading
Gear distributes fore and aft. Designs encourage roughly symmetric LCB; bulkhead placement and hatch volume are tuned accordingly.

### Heavy-load stability
Added gear lowers CG (gear usually below the paddler) → **more initial stability** when loaded. A Nordkapp feels "tippy-empty, solid-loaded" in the usual paddler description. This is design intent.

### Windage when empty
When unloaded, an expedition kayak rides high, exposing more deck to wind. Skeg deployment compensates.

### Efficiency at cruise
The cruise-speed drag curve matters more than for a day boat — expedition paddlers are often doing 25–40 km days, where 10% extra drag means half an hour extra on the water.

---

## Drag signature

- Cruise speed typical: 2.2–2.8 m/s.
- Fr at cruise: 0.30–0.37.
- Frictional drag dominates up to ~2.5 m/s; wave-making takes over above.
- Long LWL pushes the steep rise in wave-making further right on the curve.

---

## Stability signature

Loaded expedition kayak typically has:
- GM_T: 0.10–0.20 m (moderate).
- AVS: 110°–140°.
- Strong secondary, usable to edge the boat against crosswinds.

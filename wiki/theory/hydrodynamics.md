# Hydrodynamics (fundamentals)

The moving-water half of the problem. This page sets up the dimensionless numbers and flow-regime thinking we'll need for [drag-and-resistance.md](drag-and-resistance.md), [wave-making.md](wave-making.md), and [seakeeping.md](seakeeping.md).

---

## 1. What a paddler actually feels

When a kayak moves at speed $U$ through water, the hull experiences:

1. **Hydrostatic** pressure (ambient buoyancy) — balanced by weight, already covered in [hydrostatics.md](hydrostatics.md).
2. **Dynamic** pressure variations along the hull — producing drag, trim changes, squat, and wave-making.
3. **Viscous shear** on the wetted surface — producing skin friction.
4. **Separated / turbulent flow** where the boundary layer detaches — producing form drag.
5. **Free-surface waves** generated by the hull — producing wave-making resistance and leaving a wake.

Only (1) and (5) have "kayak-scale intuition"; the rest are where things get subtle.

---

## 2. Governing equations

The full problem is the Navier–Stokes equations for a viscous incompressible fluid with a free surface:

$$
\rho\left( \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla \mathbf{u} \right) = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + \rho \mathbf{g}
$$
$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0
$$

With a free surface $\eta(x,y,t)$:
- Kinematic BC: $\partial_t \eta + \mathbf{u}_s \cdot \nabla \eta = w$ at $z = \eta$.
- Dynamic BC: pressure $= $ atmospheric at $z = \eta$ (ignoring surface tension at this scale).

Nobody solves this directly inside a design loop. We simplify aggressively.

## 3. Dimensionless numbers

### Reynolds number
$$
Re = \frac{U L}{\nu}
$$
where $\nu$ is kinematic viscosity (~1.0 × 10⁻⁶ m²/s at 20 °C for water).

- Measures inertia vs. viscosity.
- Governs the **boundary layer** — laminar vs turbulent transition, and hence skin friction.

Kayak scale:
- $L \sim 5$ m, $U \sim 2$ m/s → $Re \sim 10^7$.
- Flow is turbulent over most of the hull. (Transition typically occurs within the first ~0.1 m; after that, turbulent boundary layer dominates.)

### Froude number
$$
Fr = \frac{U}{\sqrt{g\,L}}
$$

- Measures inertia vs. gravity (the restoring force on surface waves).
- Governs **wave-making resistance**.

Kayak scale:
- $L_\text{WL} \sim 5$ m, $U \sim 1.5$–$2.5$ m/s → $Fr \sim 0.2$–$0.36$.
- At $Fr \approx 0.40$–$0.45$, wave-making becomes dominant; this is what old texts call "hull speed."
- K1 racing paddlers can exceed $Fr \sim 0.45$ briefly; surfskis can do it on downwind runs. Most touring paddlers spend most of their time at $Fr \sim 0.25$–$0.30$.

**$Fr$ is dimensionless speed.** Two hulls at the same Froude number experience geometrically-similar wave systems. This is why we compare hulls by $Fr$, not by knots.

### Froude number based on displacement volume

$$
Fr_\nabla = \frac{U}{\sqrt{g\, V^{1/3}}}
$$

Useful for comparing hulls of different length but similar displacement — commonly used for rowing shells and surfskis.

### Weber / surface tension

At kayak scale, surface tension is negligible for hull-scale waves; it matters for capillary waves on the free surface but doesn't affect hull resistance.

## 4. Flow regimes for a kayak hull

| Regime | Fr range | Dominant resistance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Viscous-dominated** | Fr ≲ 0.2 | Skin friction | Very slow paddling / displacement at rest |
| **Displacement cruising** | Fr 0.2–0.35 | Skin friction + growing wave-making | Most touring / sea-kayak cruising |
| **Transition** | Fr 0.35–0.45 | Wave-making rising sharply | "Hull speed" region |
| **Above hull speed** | Fr 0.45–0.55 | Wave-making dominant | K1 sprint, surfski downwind — possible but expensive |
| **Semi-planing** | Fr > 0.5 (rare) | Hydrodynamic lift begins | Requires hull shape optimized for it; not typical for kayaks |
| **Planing** | Fr ≫ 1 (irrelevant) | Different regime | Powerboats |

Kayaks live in rows 1–4. Planing kayaks exist (some whitewater boats) but they're not what we're optimizing for.

## 5. Boundary layer basics

When water flows over the hull:

1. Water immediately in contact sticks (no-slip condition).
2. Far from the hull, water moves at free-stream velocity $U$.
3. In between, velocity transitions through a thin **boundary layer** of thickness $\delta$.

$\delta$ grows with distance $x$ along the hull:
- **Laminar:** $\delta \sim 5 x / \sqrt{Re_x}$
- **Turbulent:** $\delta \sim 0.37 x / Re_x^{1/5}$

Transition from laminar to turbulent happens around $Re_x \sim 5 \times 10^5$, so within ~0.1 m for a kayak at cruising speed. Roughness, incidence, and curvature all shift this earlier.

### Skin friction coefficient

For a flat plate in turbulent flow (ITTC 1957 line):
$$
C_F = \frac{0.075}{(\log_{10} Re - 2)^2}
$$

At kayak scale $Re \sim 10^7$, $C_F \approx 0.003$.

Skin-friction drag:
$$
R_F = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho U^2 \cdot S \cdot C_F
$$
with $S$ = wetted surface area.

This is the dominant drag at most paddling speeds, and the reason wetted surface area matters so much.

### Form (pressure) drag

Beyond flat-plate friction, the hull's shape induces pressure gradients that extract additional drag — the "form drag" or "form factor" $k$:

$$
R_V = (1 + k) R_F
$$

For slender hulls (kayaks), $k \sim 0.1$–$0.2$; fatter hulls can be 0.3+.

## 6. Free-surface waves

Gravity waves on deep water obey the dispersion relation:
$$
\omega^2 = g k
$$

Phase speed:
$$
c = \sqrt{g/k} = \sqrt{g \lambda / (2\pi)}
$$

A boat moving at speed $U$ generates waves whose phase speed matches $U$. The wavelength of these "bow waves":
$$
\lambda = \frac{2\pi U^2}{g}
$$

Example: at $U = 2$ m/s, $\lambda \approx 2.6$ m.

When the boat's waterline length equals the bow wavelength, $Fr = 1/\sqrt{2\pi} \approx 0.40$ — the old "hull speed" from $1.34 \sqrt{L_\text{ft}}$ in knots. Not a wall, but the region where wave-making rises steeply.

More in [wave-making.md](wave-making.md).

### Kelvin wake

A moving disturbance on deep water generates a steady wake pattern with a characteristic half-angle of $\arcsin(1/3) \approx 19.47°$, independent of speed (Kelvin, 1887). The wake contains transverse waves (aligned perpendicular to motion) and diverging waves (V-shaped).

A kayak's wake is faithful to this pattern. At low Fr the wake is small; at higher Fr the transverse wave system becomes prominent and its crests visibly align with the hull's length.

## 7. Induced drag from lift?

Kayaks are generally *not* lifting bodies — the hull is a displacement body, not a wing. Skegs and rudders *are* lifting surfaces when the boat yaws; they generate induced drag proportional to the square of side-force. Small contributor at typical paddling attitudes.

## 8. Potential flow, thin-ship, panel methods

When we want more than empirical formulas but less than full CFD:

- **Potential flow** — assume inviscid, irrotational flow ($\nabla \times \mathbf{u} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{u} = \nabla\phi$). Gives wave-making resistance for free surface. No viscosity → must add skin friction separately.
- **Thin-ship theory (Michell, 1898)** — further linearizes the free-surface boundary condition and assumes hull is a small perturbation to a flat plane. Closed-form integral for wave-making resistance. Fast. Accurate for slender hulls.
- **Panel methods (boundary element)** — discretize hull with source/dipole panels, solve for flow. More general than thin-ship. Rankine or Havelock Green's functions.

ArcasBoat's drag estimator starts with **ITTC friction + Michell thin-ship wave-making**, with a form factor for residual drag. See [drag-and-resistance.md](drag-and-resistance.md).

## 9. What ArcasBoat does (and doesn't) simulate

**Does:**
- Empirical frictional drag (ITTC 1957).
- Thin-ship wave-making (Michell).
- Simple form-factor correction.
- Quasi-static trim / heel equilibrium.

**Doesn't (yet):**
- Full unsteady CFD.
- Vortex shedding, separated flow.
- Wave-body coupling in seaways (phase 3+).
- Paddler-hull dynamic coupling.
- Spray and non-linear free-surface effects (surf, breaking waves).
